An inventory cost allocation system for a multi-warehouse distribution network is generating incorrect valuations. The system - residing in /app/src/ - comprises seven Java files that coordinate to compute unit costs via weighted average costing, allocate shared logistics expenses across product lines, convert currency for international shipments, track inventory movements between warehouses, and produce period-end valuation reports.

Components in /app/src/ include CostAllocationEngine.java which orchestrates the full computation pipeline and aggregates final valuations, WeightedAverageCostCalculator.java which computes running weighted average costs per SKU as inventory moves in and out, LogisticsExpenseAllocator.java which distributes shared costs like freight and handling across units proportionally by either weight or volume, CurrencyConverter.java which handles multi-currency purchase orders and converts all costs to the reporting currency using mid-market rates, WarehouseTransferProcessor.java which tracks inter-warehouse movements and ensures cost basis follows the units, InventoryDataParser.java which reads and validates input transaction records, and ReportWriter.java which formats and outputs the final valuation report.

End-of-month inventory reports don't reconcile with the general ledger. Accountants notice the total inventory value sometimes differs from expectations by amounts that seem random but are consistently off. SKUs with high transaction volumes show larger discrepancies than those with fewer movements. International purchase orders - especially those from suppliers paid in Euros or Yen - occasionally have unit costs that don't match invoice totals when verified manually. The weighted average calculation produces values that seem close but drift further from expected as the period progresses. Logistics expenses allocated to certain product lines don't match the proportional share based on the documented allocation method. Inter-warehouse transfers occasionally result in the receiving warehouse showing slightly different unit costs than the sending warehouse had recorded.

Correct behavior requires that the weighted average cost equals the sum of (previous_quantity times previous_avg_cost plus incoming_quantity times incoming_unit_cost) divided by (previous_quantity plus incoming_quantity), with the result truncated to exactly 4 decimal places - no rounding, just truncation. Logistics expenses must be allocated proportionally using the allocation_basis column where W means weight-based and V means volume-based, with each SKU's share being its basis_value divided by the total basis_value for all SKUs in that allocation batch, multiplied by the total_expense amount, then truncated to 2 decimal places. Currency conversion uses the rate from the rates file matching the transaction_date and currency_code, multiplying foreign_amount by the rate and truncating to 4 decimal places. Inter-warehouse transfers must carry the exact unit_cost from the source warehouse without modification. The valuation report must show final_unit_cost truncated to 4 decimal places and total_value truncated to 2 decimal places, where total_value equals final_quantity times final_unit_cost.

The transactions file has columns sku, transaction_date (yyyy-MM-dd), transaction_type (PURCHASE or TRANSFER_IN or TRANSFER_OUT or SALE), quantity, unit_cost, warehouse_id, currency_code, and allocation_batch_id. The rates file has columns effective_date, currency_code, and rate_to_usd. The expenses file has columns batch_id, total_expense, and allocation_basis where allocation_basis is either W or V. Each SKU has an associated weight_kg and volume_m3 defined in a lookup embedded in the code. The output CSV requires a header row and columns sku, warehouse_id, final_quantity, final_unit_cost, allocated_expense, and total_value in that order, with rows sorted by sku ascending and then warehouse_id ascending within each sku.
